## Naive Bayes

We continue to introduce algorithms for solving classification tasks. This lesson introduces a probability model called the Bayesian classifier. Bayesian classifier is a general name for a group of classification algorithms. These algorithms are all based on Bayes' theorem, so they are together called Bayesian classifiers. Before introducing Bayes' theorem, let us first tell a story. From 2015 to 2020, a woman surnamed Li bought about 900 flight-delay insurance policies by using her own analysis of whether flights would be delayed, and she finally received more than 3 million yuan in compensation. You could really call it "flight delay makes you rich". Of course, this operation itself is not the key point we want to discuss. Our question is: how did Ms. Li decide whether to buy delay insurance? The main reasons for flight delay are weather, including both departure and arrival weather, airport, including both departure and arrival airport, and airline. Because Ms. Li had worked in aviation service before, she had ways to get data related to airports and airlines, and weather data was easier to get. After gathering the related data and using Bayes' theorem, she could calculate the probability that the current flight would be delayed and decide whether to buy delay insurance. Later, Ms. Li also bought tickets under made-up identities and bought a large amount of insurance, around 30 to 40 policies for each identity. In this way, once a flight was delayed, she could ask the insurance company for compensation. So, what we want to discuss is how Bayes' theorem uses existing data to calculate the probability of flight delay.

### Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' theorem is an important theorem in probability theory. It describes how we can start from subjective experience or known facts, and then use collected sample data as evidence to update our understanding of the probability of an event. The mathematical formula of Bayes' theorem is:

$$
P(A \vert B) = \frac{P(B \vert A)}{P(B)} \cdot P(A)
$$

Here, $\small{P(A)}$ is the **prior probability** of event $\small{A}$. We can understand it as known facts or subjective experience. $\small{P(B \vert A)}$ is the conditional probability that event $\small{B}$ happens when event $\small{A}$ has happened, and it is usually also called the **likelihood**. $\small{P(B)}$ is the total probability that event $\small{B}$ happens. These two probabilities can be obtained from the sample data we collect as evidence. $\small{P(A \vert B)}$ is the conditional probability that event $\small{A}$ happens when event $\small{B}$ has happened. That is our updated understanding of the probability of event $\small{A}$ after collecting sample data, and it is called the **posterior probability**. Bayes' theorem tells us an important fact: we can start from known facts or subjective experience, and then use collected evidence to update our understanding of the probability of some event. To say it simply, **we can use known facts and collected evidence to infer an unknown truth**.

Back to the flight-delay insurance example above. Suppose this flight goes from Chengdu Shuangliu International Airport to Beijing Capital International Airport, the airline is Sichuan Airlines, the departure weather is rainy with light rain, temperature 8 degrees Celsius, and northeast wind at level 2, and the arrival weather is sunny with temperature 4 degrees Celsius and northwest wind at level 2. To make Bayes' theorem easier to understand, we simplify the conditions a little here and only keep precipitation information in the weather for now, without considering other factors such as temperature and wind speed. Then the theorem above becomes:

$$
P(\text{delay} \vert \text{departure airport=Shuangliu, arrival airport=Capital, departure weather=light rain, arrival weather=sunny, airline=Sichuan Airlines}) = \\\\
\frac{P(\text{departure airport=Shuangliu, arrival airport=Capital, departure weather=light rain, arrival weather=sunny, airline=Sichuan Airlines} \vert \text{delay})}{P(\text{departure airport=Shuangliu, arrival airport=Capital, departure weather=light rain, arrival weather=sunny, airline=Sichuan Airlines})} \cdot P(\text{delay})
$$

The left side of the formula above is the probability of delay for the current flight that Ms. Li wants to know. The part on the right side is actually historical data and current information. The key to calculating this probability is finding the likelihood, that is, the value of:

$$
P(\text{departure airport=Shuangliu, arrival airport=Capital, departure weather=light rain, arrival weather=sunny, airline=Sichuan Airlines} \vert \text{delay})
$$

So how do we calculate this conditional probability?

### Naive Bayes

Naive Bayes is a classification algorithm based on Bayes' theorem and the assumption that features are conditionally independent. It is widely used because it is simple and efficient. The key is the word "naive". It means the assumption of conditional independence of features. This assumption says that when the class is fixed, the features used for classification are independent from each other. This assumption makes learning with naive Bayes possible.

Suppose we have a feature set $\small{X = \{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\}}$ and a class $\small{C}$. Naive Bayes assumes:

$$
P(X \vert C) = P(x_1 \vert C) \cdot P(x_2 \vert C) \cdot \ldots \cdot P(x_n \vert C)
$$

This assumption greatly simplifies the computational complexity. We only need to calculate the probability of each feature under a given class, and do not need to consider the interaction between features. In the flight-delay insurance example above, we can calculate the likelihood in the following way:

$$
P(\text{departure airport=Shuangliu, arrival airport=Capital, departure weather=light rain, arrival weather=sunny, airline=Sichuan Airlines} \vert \text{delay}) = \\\\
P(\text{departure airport=Shuangliu} \vert \text{delay}) \times P(\text{arrival airport=Capital} \vert \text{delay}) \times P(\text{departure weather=light rain} \vert \text{delay}) \times P(\text{arrival weather=sunny} \vert \text{delay}) \times P(\text{airline=Sichuan Airlines} \vert \text{delay})
$$

### Algorithm Principle

#### Training stage

In the training stage, naive Bayes first needs to calculate the prior probability of each class and the conditional probability of each feature under each class.

1. **Calculate prior probability**:

$$
P(C_{i}) = \frac{n_{i}}{n}
$$

Here, $\small{C_{i}}$ is a class, $\small{n_{i}}$ is the number of samples in class $\small{C_{i}}$, and $\small{n}$ is the total number of samples.

2. **Calculate conditional probability**:

$$
P(x_{j} \vert C_{i}) = \frac{n_{i,j}}{n_{i}}
$$

Here, $\small{n_{i,j}}$ is the number of times that feature $\small{x_j}$ appears in class $\small{C_i}$.

#### Prediction stage

In the prediction stage, given a sample $\small{X}$ to classify, naive Bayes calculates the posterior probability that it belongs to each class by the following steps:

$$
P(C_{i} \vert X) = \frac{P(X \vert C_{i})}{P(X)} \cdot P(C_{i})
$$

In the formula above, $\small{P(X)}$ is a constant for every class, so we can ignore it. Then with the independence assumption we have:

$$
P(C_{i} \vert X) \propto P(C_{i}) \cdot P(x_1 \vert C_{i}) \cdot P(x_2 \vert C_{i}) \cdot \ldots \cdot P(x_n \vert C_{i})
$$

In this way, we can choose the class with the highest posterior probability as the prediction result.

#### Code implementation

We still use the iris dataset as an example. Following the principle explained above, let us implement a naive Bayes classifier with NumPy. We still start by loading the data.

```python
from sklearn.datasets import load_iris
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split

iris = load_iris()
X, y = iris.data, iris.target
X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(X, y, train_size=0.8, random_state=3)
```

In the training stage, we need to get class labels and their prior probabilities. Also, we need to calculate the likelihoods. The calculation of likelihoods uses the "naive" assumption mentioned above. Here we do a simple discretization of the continuous feature values in the iris dataset first. Do not worry first about whether this treatment is fully reasonable.

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd


def naive_bayes_fit(X, y):
    """
    Train a naive Bayes classifier.
    :param X: sample features
    :param y: sample labels
    :return: a tuple of prior probabilities and likelihoods
    """
    # Calculate prior probabilities.
    clazz_labels, clazz_counts = np.unique(y, return_counts=True)
    prior_probs = pd.Series({k: v / y.size for k, v in zip(clazz_labels, clazz_counts)})
    # Copy the array to create a new array.
    X = np.copy(X)
    # Dictionary used to save likelihood calculation results.
    likelihoods = {}
    for j in range(X.shape[1]):  # Loop over features.
        # Put feature values into equal-width bins.
        X[:, j] = pd.cut(X[:, j], bins=5, labels=np.arange(1, 6))
        for i in prior_probs.index:
            # Split data by class label and count feature values.
            x_prime = X[y == i, j]
            x_values, x_counts = np.unique(x_prime, return_counts=True)
            for k, value in enumerate(x_values):  # Loop over different feature values.
                # Save the likelihood in the dictionary.
                likelihoods[(i, j, value)] = x_counts[k] / x_prime.size
    return prior_probs, likelihoods
```

Calling the function above gives us a tuple. After unpacking it, we get the prior probabilities of the class labels $\small{C_i}$ and the likelihood that the $\small{j}$-th feature takes some value `value` in class $\small{C_i}$. The first is a `Series` object and the second is a `dict` object, as shown below.

```python
p_ci, p_x_ci = naive_bayes_fit(X_train, y_train)

print('likelihoods: ', p_x_ci, sep='\n')
```

In the prediction stage, we use the prior probabilities and likelihoods returned by the function above to calculate posterior probabilities, and then use the class with the largest posterior probability as the predicted class label.

```python
def naive_bayes_predict(X, p_ci, p_x_ci):
    """
    Naive Bayes classifier prediction
    :param X: sample features
    :param p_ci: prior probabilities
    :param p_x_ci: likelihoods
    :return: predicted labels
    """
    # Put feature values into equal-width bins.
    X = np.copy(X)
    for j in range(X.shape[1]):
        X[:, j] = pd.cut(X[:, j], bins=5, labels=np.arange(1, 6))
    # A 2D array used to save posterior probabilities for each sample and each class.
    results = np.zeros((X.shape[0], p_ci.size))
    clazz_labels = p_ci.index.values
    for k in range(X.shape[0]):
        for i, label in enumerate(clazz_labels):
            # Get prior probability.
            prob = p_ci.loc[label]
            # Calculate posterior probability after getting feature data.
            for j in range(X.shape[1]):
                # If there is no matching likelihood, use 0.
                prob *= p_x_ci.get((i, j, X[k, j]), 0)
            results[k, i] = prob
    # Choose the class with the highest probability for each sample.
    return clazz_labels[results.argmax(axis=1)]
```

The two functions above are mainly to help everyone understand how naive Bayes works. In actual work, we still recommend using the classes provided by the `naive_bayes` module in scikit-learn to create naive Bayes models. This module has five variants of naive Bayes. Each variant is for different kinds of data and feature distributions. The differences are shown in the table below.

| Classifier       | Feature type         | Main assumption                                                |
| ---------------- | -------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `BernoulliNB`    | binary features      | features follow Bernoulli distribution                         |
| `CategoricalNB`  | categorical features | features follow multinomial distribution, often used for categorical data |
| `ComplementNB`   | count features       | uses complement probability, often used for imbalanced datasets |
| `GaussianNB`     | continuous features  | features follow Gaussian distribution                          |
| `MultinomialNB`  | count features       | features follow multinomial distribution, often used for text classification |

For the iris dataset, because its features are continuous values, we can use `GaussianNB` to create the model, as shown below.

```python
from sklearn.naive_bayes import GaussianNB

model = GaussianNB()
model.fit(X_train, y_train)
y_pred = model.predict(X_test)
```

### Advantages and Disadvantages

The advantages and disadvantages of naive Bayes include:

1. **The logic is simple and easy to implement, and it is suitable for large-scale datasets**.
2. **The computational cost is small**. The probabilities needed for prediction are already prepared in the training stage. When new data comes, we only need to get the corresponding probability values and do simple calculations to get the prediction result.
3. **It is not easily affected by noise and irrelevant attributes**.

Of course, because it makes the assumption that "features are independent from each other", the weakness of naive Bayes is also very obvious. In real applications, it is hard for features to be completely independent, especially for high-dimensional data. If the correlation between features is large, the classification effect can become poor. To solve this problem, some new methods have later been developed based on naive Bayes, including semi-naive Bayes, AODE, K-dependent naive Bayes, naive Bayes network, Gaussian mixture naive Bayes, and so on. Interested readers can learn about them by themselves.

### Summary

Naive Bayes is widely used in many fields. The following are some common application scenarios:

- **Text classification**: such as spam detection and sentiment analysis.
- **Recommendation systems**: making personalized recommendations according to user behavior and preferences.
- **Medical diagnosis**: predicting diseases according to symptoms.
